Problem solving - Friday 16th July 2021

JavaScript problem solving techniques


This is a brief story about my learning journey and how I dealt with an issue that arose along the way.


I was trying to resolve some exercises from my course curriculum, when I encountered a problem that was not that simple to resolve. What I needed to resolve was finding out the average score of 4 different values given for every student on the list.

As I had been advised throughout the course about how to approach problems, I first did an analysis of the bigger picture.
“What am I really trying to accomplish here?”. Once I did this, I then decided to start breaking the problem down into pieces, therefore, I wrote pseudocode as a guide.
Pseudocode is written in plain english and basically you figure out every step needed in order to reach the result.

A great analogy to this will be baking a cake. You want to do the steps in the right order so you will be able to achieve the desired result.
Example, what temperature your oven needs to be, do you mix the dry ingredients first or the wet ones, how long does your cake take to be ready, ect.

Once I wrote this, I needed to figure out the necessary ingredients and quantities to make this cake. So I started writing my code in small pieces and gathering all of the necessary elements.
When I wasn’t sure how to achieve one of those steps, I decided to do some research online and find other people that encountered a similar situation or someone that has expertise in the area and has some learning lessons.
There are several online resources, from articles, videos, online platforms that have exercises, among others.

At this point my perspective changed from the first impression I had about the problem. At first, this challenge seemed impossible to accomplish but I ended up proving myself wrong.
I started realizing that resolving this challenge on my own was doable if only taken the right approach.


This is a reflection about the problem solving techniques applied.


I noticed that I feel confident using the pseudocode technique because breaking down the problem helps me analyze the necessary elements of code needed to achieve the result. Also, I do use google to learn further how to apply the properties or watch videos that explain so.
Then I give it a go, and try modifying it if the error message finds some syntax. I also like console.logging to learn how the different pieces of the code interact.

I find that I use the same techniques to approach the problems and this seems to work out so far. I am confident that I will be able to solve other challenges on my own since this past Sprint was a bit too challenging and I had to take a look into the proposed solutions to guide my thinking.

Keep it up everyone,
